# The Things That Get in the Way

If we want to live and love with our whole hearts, and if we want to engage with the world from a place of worthiness, we have to talk about the things that get in the way -- especially shame, fear, and our resistance to vulnerability.

*The story Bréne gave was her disastrous past talk she gave to a group of women in a country club where she was uncomfortably asked to modify her speech to fit the narratives required by the host, presenting herself in an unauthentic way. And she obliged. And this memory came back as she was preparing for another high stake public presentation.*

*She felt so uncomfortable even to recount this memory to her husband (he had no idea this happened). I could only imagine that kind of shame and unpleasantness this memory brought. And this related strongly to how I felt and chose to deal with some past memories where I was shameful about, didn't want to face them again, didn't want to talk about them again, and although often I had learned a few things from them, seeing that I couldn't even bear to think about some of those memories again, it's highly questionable if I had really grown from such experiences and had a healthy relationship with myself.*

"How-to" is a seductive shortcut...But here's the dilemma: Why is "how-to" so alluring when, truthfully, we already know "how to" yet we're still standing in the same place longing for more joy, connection, and meaning?
Because we don't talk about the things that get in the way of doing what we know is best for us.

Shame Resilience 101
1. We all have it. Shame is universal and one of the most primitive human emotions that we experience. The only people who don't experience shame lack the capacity for empathy and human connection.
2. We're all afraid to talk about shame.
3. The less we talk about shame, the more control it has over our lives.

Shame is basically the fear of being unlovable -- it's the total opposite of owning our story and feeling unworthy.

What is shame?
- Shame is the intensely painful feeling or experience of believing that we are flawed and therefore unworthy of love, belonging, and connection.
- Shame keeps worthiness away by convincing us that owning our stories will lead to people thinking less of us. 
- Shame is all about fear. We're afraid that people won't like us if they know the truth about who we are, where we come from, what we believe, how much we're struggling, or, believe it or not, how wonderful we are when soaring (sometimes it's just as hard to own our strenghts as our struggles).

Shame needs three things to grow out of control in our lives:
- secrecy
- silence
- judgment

Find someone who has earned the right to hear our story. Tell it. Shame loses power when it is spoken.

People with high levels of shame resilience share these 4 elements:
- They understand shame and recognize what messages and expectations trigger shame for them.
- They practice critical awareness by reality-checking the messages and expectations that tell us that being imperfect means being inadequate.
- They reach out and share their stories with people they trust.
- They speak shame -- they use the word shame, they talk about how they're feeling, and they ask for what they need.

Shame vs Guilt
- shame: I am bad (about who we are)
- guilt: I did something bad (about behaviors)

More often guilt drives positive changes in ways shame does not. Full of shamee or the fear of shame, we are more likely to engage in self-destructive behaviors and to attack or shame others. Shame is highly correlated with violence, aggression, depression, addiction, eating disorders, and bullying.

What does shame look like?
- move away by withdrawing, hiding, silencing ourselves, and keeping secrets
- move toward by seeking to appease and please
- move against by trying to gain power over others, by being aggressive, and by using shame to fight shame
Most people use all these -- at different times with different folks for different reasons.

All these strategies move us away from our story.
Shame is about fear, blame, and disconnection.
Story is about worthiness and embracing the imperfections that bring us courage, compassion, and connection. Own our story. 

Respond to shame in a way that doesn't exacerbate our shame. One way is to recognize when we're in shame so we can react with intention.

When we're in shame, we're not fit for human consumption. We need to get back on our emotional feet before we do/say something that we'll regret.

To kick-start your shame resilience and story-claiming, start with these questions:
1. Who do you become when you're backed into that shame corner?
2. How do you protect yourself?
3. Who do you call to work through the mean-nasties or the cry 'n' hides or the people-pleasing?
4. What's the most courageous thing you could do for yourself when you feel small and hurt?

Our stories are not meant for everyone. Ask ourselves: who has earned the right to hear my story?

We don't need love and belonging and story-catchiung from everyone in our lives, but we need it from at least one person.
